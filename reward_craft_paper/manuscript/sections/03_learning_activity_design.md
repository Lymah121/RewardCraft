# 3. Learning Activity Design

The core of this project lies in the design of a learning activity that effectively teaches Reinforcement Learning (RL) concepts while simultaneously fostering critical thinking about the ethical and societal implications of AI. This section outlines the principles guiding the design of our game-based learning activity, emphasizing the integration of ethics and the unique role of the reward function as a site for ethical inquiry.

## 3.1. Integrating Ethics and Societal Impact: Beyond the Algorithm

A defining feature of contemporary AI education is the growing consensus that teaching the technical mechanics of AI in isolation is insufficient and potentially irresponsible. To cultivate truly AI-literate citizens, educational experiences must also equip students to think critically about the societal and ethical dimensions of these powerful technologies. Our learning activity design adopts the principle of **embedded ethics**, ensuring that ethical considerations are woven into the fabric of the learning experience itself, rather than being treated as an afterthought.

The work of Williams et al. (2022) on AI + Ethics Curricula for Middle School Youth strongly advocates for this approach, demonstrating that students can effectively explore technical concepts in tandem with ethical ones. Similarly, the DAILy (Developing AI Literacy) workshop reported by Lee et al. (2021) provides empirical validation for an integrated, ethics-forward approach, showing that middle school participants are not only capable of engaging with complex ethical topics but are highly engaged in discussions about fairness and bias in AI. These findings provide a clear mandate: our tool should not shy away from presenting students with complex ethical scenarios, as these challenges can capture their attention and provide a safe, controlled environment for firsthand experience.

## 3.2. The Reward Function as a Site of Ethical Inquiry

In many real-world AI systems, ethical failures are not the result of a "bug" in the code but rather the logical outcome of an algorithm successfully optimizing for a poorly specified or incomplete objective function. A game-based RL tool provides a unique and powerful medium for making this abstract concept concrete, interactive, and understandable.

The core of a Reinforcement Learning system involves an agent learning to maximize a cumulative reward signal. The design of that reward signal—what to reward, what to punish, and by how much—is a fundamentally human choice that encodes a set of values and priorities into the system. As illustrated by the `TrainYourSnakeAI` paper, when students created a "negative configuration" by rewarding the snake for simply staying alive, the agent learned unintended and undesirable behavior (moving in circles, ignoring food). This demonstrates a profound connection: the technical interface for designing the reward function in an RL game is not just a technical tool; it is an **ethics tool**. It is the primary mechanism through which students can directly engage with concepts like algorithmic bias, specification gaming, and unintended consequences. The "Training Arena" can thus be reconceptualized as an "Ethics Arena."

## 3.3. Designing Ethical Gameplay Challenges

This reframing opens up a rich space for designing gameplay challenges that are simultaneously technical and ethical. The learning activity will present students with scenarios where the technical task of optimization is inextricably linked to an ethical dilemma. For example:

*   **The Delivery Drone Challenge:** Students might be tasked with training a delivery drone agent. A simple reward function might only reward the agent for speed of delivery. Students would quickly discover that the agent learns to fly in a straight line through city parks, potentially disturbing wildlife or people, because that is the most efficient path. The game would then pose a new challenge: "How would you change the reward function to make the agent both efficient and considerate of its environment?" This would require students to add penalties for flying through sensitive areas or bonuses for adhering to designated flight paths, transforming a simple optimization task into a rich socio-technical design problem.
*   **Resource Allocation Scenarios:** Another challenge could involve an agent managing resources in a simulated environment. A reward function focused solely on maximizing one resource might lead to the depletion of another, or unfair distribution among different simulated populations. Students would then need to redesign the reward function to balance multiple objectives, such as efficiency, fairness, and sustainability.

Through these types of challenges, students will learn not just *that* bias or unintended consequences can exist, but *how* they can emerge from the technical choices a designer makes in defining the reward structure. This hands-on experience with complex trade-offs involved in real-world AI design directly addresses the pedagogical goals outlined in the AI ethics curricula. The learning activity will encourage iterative experimentation, observation of consequences, and critical reflection, empowering students to become more thoughtful and responsible designers of AI systems.
