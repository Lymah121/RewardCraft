Abstract

Keywords

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is growing rapidly in our society and
is now apparent in our day-to-day lives. With the recent burst
of interest in AI, many individuals and children may view AI as
something mystic and magical. It is important to demystify and
introduce to them how AI is made and works. To address this need,
we developed a software application that allows children to specify
the parameters used by a Reinforcement Learning (RL) algorithm.
Then students experience how RL is used to train an AI model
to play the game “Snake.” This software tool was tested with 71
middle school-age students. Here, we describe the design of the
TrainYourSnakeAI application, the approach we used to introduce
the associated ideas to middle school children, and how we assessed
student learning. Qualitative data collected from students are presented and discussed. We surveyed their knowledge of AI before
and after using the application. In this work, our research questions
were: (RQ1) How can we create an engaging tool to teach reinforcement learning? and (RQ2) Does using our application foster a
stronger understanding of reinforcement learning in children? Our
findings indicate that students were able to understand the functionality of reward functions and how agents can learn from the
environment using the concept of RL. We found that out of the 51
students who were not previously familiar with RL, 40 were able to
provide adequate descriptions of RL after using TrainYourSnakeAI.

Introduction and Motivation

In today’s rapidly evolving world, artificial intelligence (AI) is becoming an integral part of daily life [10, 19], influencing even our
children’s early interactions through platforms like social media,
games, Alexa, Bixby, and Siri, etc. Thus, the younger generation
needs to understand, assess, and familiarize themselves with AI systems [11, 24]. This knowledge can help them evaluate information,
improve computational thinking skills, and prepare for future job
markets [2, 12].
AI education can foster critical thinking and problem-solving
skills. With the usage of AI concepts, students can learn to analyze
complex problems, break them down into manageable components,
and devise creative solutions. This analytical mindset nurtures their
ability to adapt to various challenges, which is a crucial skill in
an era defined by uncertainty and rapid change. In addition, AI
education cultivates digital literacy and technological fluency. In an
increasingly digital world, understanding the fundamentals of AI
empowers students to navigate and harness technology effectively.
By exploring AI applications and concepts, students are inspired to
think outside the box and develop innovative solutions to real-world
problems.
In this paper, we describe the design and implementation of the
TrainYourSnakeAI game for Artificial Intelligence (AI) education
and more specifically on Reinforcement Learning (RL). Our application allows middle school students to learn about RL by training an
agent to play the game Snake. Students can see how their training

Related Work

Integrating AI education into K-12 education is becoming increasingly necessary due to how AI is becoming more commonly used in
various fields. For example, AI in the education field can help personalize learning, letting students learn at their speed and meeting
their specific needs.
As such, AI researchers for K-12 working group developed an
online resource directory for teachers with access to AI-related
videos, demonstrations, software, and activity descriptions that
they can integrate into their lesson plans [13, 22] and designed
AI technologies centered on learners that foster increased user
understanding of AI [12]. Recently, Shuchi Grover introduced the
early AI and CS education efforts in K-12 AI education, highlighted
critical design issues, and offered guidance for future initiatives to
effectively integrate AI learning into K-12 curricula [3, 8].
In the literature, many game-based applications [13, 29] and
tools [14] were introduced, like a social robot-based application [5],
game-based approach [18, 25], and a block-based programming
tool [7] to teach students aged 10–16 with focused AI concepts
through interactive and project-based learning activities [15] in
K-12 education.
There has been a growing interest in teaching Reinforcement
Learning (RL) specifically at various educational levels, from elementary school to undergraduate studies. The AI4K12 guidelines
emphasize the importance of “Learning” within the “Five Big Ideas
in AI,” including reinforcement learning as a key machine learning concept for grades 9-12, where students learn how algorithms
adjust internal models through environmental interaction and feedback, highlighting the necessity of teaching RL to K-12 students [1].
Many researchers have explored methods to introduce RL using
accessible tools and activities tailored for teenagers [15]. Furthermore, RL can also be used in various applications and fields which
demonstrates the increasing significance of AI literacy [23, 27].
In recent years, RL has been introduced through digital game
designs. For example, Voulgari et al.’s work on ArtBot teaches students at the primary and middle school level to explore the impact
of parameters, rewards, and training data on the learned behavior
of AI agents through these games [25, 28]. The ArtBot game is designed to introduce two important concepts: supervised learning
and RL. The first part of this game introduces supervised learning
by allowing the player to train an AI helper to find art objects.
During the second part of the game, players, along with their AI
helper, navigate dungeons to collect stolen art objects, learning
RL concepts by assigning rewards to objects and avoiding traps,
adjusting parameters like exploration and exploitation rates, and
observing the AI’s behavior using a Q-learning algorithm on an
8x8 grid while modifying reward settings with sliders. This work
demonstrates an interactive approach to introduce children to RL.
Quiloan et al.’s work on the PlayGrid project developed three
prototypes to teach RL to children, with a focus on creating tools
that can benefit young children who may not have coding knowledge [18]. PlayGrid’s final prototype involved a hardware device

Design of TrainYourSnakeAI Game

Our web application exposes children to RL. We chose RL because
we thought it would be an intuitive concept for students to understand, while also showing them an important concept in AI. While
designing our application, we kept in mind two of the “Five Big
Ideas in AI” [22]: Representation and Reasoning, and Learning. The
main idea of our software is for children to train their own AI to
play the game Snake and observe how the snake reacts based off
different reward functions specified by the user.

3.1

Game-play

Our application first asks the user to input their first name, last
initial, and grade level to capture data. We then give them a brief
introduction to RL. Then, the children are asked to briefly play the
Snake game themselves. Finally, they are taken to a page called the
“Training Arena” where they can play around with different reward
functions for the snake and train it in different ways.
3.1.1 Introduction to RL. Once the child enters their information,
they are taken to a page where they are briefly introduced to RL.
This page has text explaining that “If you reward a chicken when
it does a back flip, it will do more back flips. Also, if you punish a
chicken when it misbehaves, it won’t misbehave as often. Think
of training your AI like this. This method is called RL. You will
be training your AI to play the game Snake. But first. . . Play the
snake game yourself!.” Additionally, we added an image of a chick
to this page to grab the attention of the children and make the
software more playful and relaxed. The introduction paragraph is
not a formal definition of RL by any means but compares RL to
how a chicken (or any biological organism) learns. The idea was to
provide an easy-to-understand and intuitive definition for children,
while not straying from the formal definition.

3.1.2 Snake. The Snake game with RL involves an agent that
learns to play by interacting with the game environment, receiving
rewards and penalties, and continuously improving its strategy
through repeated play and learning algorithms [21, 26].
The game environment includes the snake, food, and the game
board. The snake’s objective is to eat the food that appears randomly
while avoiding collisions with itself and the board’s boundaries.
An RL agent learns to play the game by interacting with the environment, receiving the current state, and taking actions (moving
up, down, left, or right). The agent gets rewards for eating food
and penalties for collisions. It develops a policy to choose actions
based on the state and uses algorithms such as Q-learning to learn
and improve its policy over time. By playing numerous games and
updating its policy, the agent learns to make better decisions, such
as finding efficient paths to the food and avoiding collisions.
Nokia is well known for including the Snake game on its phones
starting in 1997 [4]. Though the game’s concept dates back to arcade
games in the 1970s, the Nokia version played a key role in popularizing it globally [6, 16]. The highly successful Nokia 3310, launched
in late 2000, further cemented Snake’s iconic status. The game became a cultural phenomenon and has inspired many remakes and
adaptations for modern devices[20].
We decided to let the children play the Snake game themselves
for those who are not familiar with it or who have not played it
recently.

Technical Details

Our application is built with Python using Flask as our web framework with a SQLite database.
The RL agent that we used is from [17] with some modifications. By dynamically changing the agent’s reward functions (i.e.,
a variable for reward, a variable for staying alive, and a variable
for dying), the AI exhibits a drastic change in the learned behavior,
and we used this as the premise of our application.
To provide an overview on the technical aspects of the AI agent
being used in our software: A state vector representing the current
snake’s environment is given to the model’s predict function, where
we are given back an action to take, also a vector. This action is
then performed inside the game and the result is evaluated. There
is now a different state vector for the environment, since an action
was just performed inside the game.
The model then updates based off of these variables so that the
next time it is called to predict, it now has more information on
the best move to take. The model uses a Feed First Neural Network
with 11 input variables (a state vector representing the current
environment), 256 hidden layers, and 3 output layers (the action
vector giving us the optimal action to take). This model is trained
repeatedly, with the goal to train it sufficiently to where you provide
a current environment (state vector with 11 values) and it will give
you an optimal action to take (action vector with three values)
based off its environment.
When the user initializes a training, the AI agent begins training
on the server side. We facilitate the real time communication from
the server to the client with web sockets. Streams of data are sent
from our backend to a JavaScript frontend, allowing the user to see
the training process in real time.
The source code for our application can be found at [9].

4.1

Learning Activities

In our table for this software, we had four computers running our
application locally. The authors spent time with the children discussing the software and provided guidance if needed. The children
were first briefly introduced to the application by one of the authors. Afterwards, they started using the application. Once they
were done, the children completed the post-survey form.

4.2

Data Gathered

We collected data from the following sources: pre-survey, postsurvey, screen/audio recording, and automated data collection from
our software. The pre-survey consisted of a universal questionnaire
that was filled out by all the students who participated in the “AI
expo” study. The pre-survey consisted of questions that gathered
data on their current understanding of AI. The post-survey we
used was designed specifically for our application and was given to
only the students who interacted with our software. Screen/audio
recordings recorded participant interaction with our application;
however, these were not used in the analysis of our research. Finally,
we also gathered application-specific data by saving the following
information into our database: user information and metrics on the
AI models (snakes) that the user created. The application-specific
data provided statistical information on the use of our application,
not student learning.

Results

We focused primarily on the results of the post-survey because we
believe that they brought the most insight from the different sources
of data we gathered. Moreover, we also connected the pre-survey
data to the post-survey data to evaluate the conceptual difference
in understanding RL.

Study Design

This web-based application TrainYourSnakeAI was tested at a public
STEM charter school in a major city in the state of Texas. The
participants were middle school students (in 6th through 8th grade)
who signed up and attended the after-school sessions. There was
a total of 122 participants. However, of those 122 children, only
71 of them used our software due to time constraints. This study
consisted of two 3-hour sessions over the course of two days. This
research took place in what we called an “AI expo,” where there
were seven applications (including TrainYourSnakeAI ) that exposed
children to different concepts in AI. There were usually 3-7 students
per table where an application was being demoed. Once the children
were done using a project, they would rotate on to the next one.

(RQ2) Does using our application foster a
stronger understanding of reinforcement
learning in children?

To measure the student’s understanding of RL, we first analyzed
the responses to “When did you think you should reward the snake?
(choose any number of options).” The correct answer to this item is
“when it eats the food.” Students were allowed to choose multiple
responses and 54 correctly selected only the correct answer, as
shown in Figure 3. An additional 14 students selected “when eating
the food,” but also incorrectly selected “when it stays alive.”
Also, another item asked, “Do you think RL is similar to how a
dog learns?” We found that 64 of the 71 students reported that RL
is similar to how a dog learns. We are aware that this question
could have been positively biased given the introduction of RL that
was provided to them in the “Introduction” page of our application.
However, we believe that it was a combination of all the aspects of
our software that instantiated these responses.
Next, we directly mapped our final post-survey item “I would
describe RL as...” to pre-survey responses as we felt the combination
of the open-ended nature of this question and the data from the
pre-survey would provide insight. The open-ended responses were
coded into four categories as follows:
• Reward and/or punishment: This is the most accurate of
the four categories. The children answered the question correctly by describing RL with the keywords reward and/or
punishment.

Discussion

Overall, most students had fun interacting with TrainYourSnakeAI
as indicated by the responses to the post-survey item “Did you have
fun training your own AI?” Most of the students (81.7%) responded
that they had fun. Furthermore, none of the students responded
“no.” This suggests that most of the students enjoyed interacting
with our application.
As seen in Table 3, from the “No RL” group, 21 students were
able to accurately describe RL using the keywords rewards and/or
punishment. We also found that 19 children compared RL to the
natural learning process of biological organisms. We include those
19 children as having described RL accurately. The purpose of our
application was to expose to children RL and we found that 40 of
the students who were not previously familiar with the AI concept
were able to describe it to a certain extent. If we move our attention
to the category “RL” (students who were already familiar with RL
before interacting with our application), we see that there were a
total of 18 students who accurately described RL out of 20.
We performed a Fisher’s Exact test for the results because the
data did not fulfill the conditions of the two-proportion z-test. The
results indicated that there were no significant differences between
the “RL” and “No RL” groups. Therefore, it seems that both groups
gave similar amounts of responses in regard to accurately describing
RL. We believe that there can be no concrete conclusions with
comparing the tables of data. The pre-survey question used to
categorize if the child was familiar with RL was “yes or no” fashion.
More insight would have been achieved if the question had been in
the open-ended manner of the post-survey question used to describe
RL as shown in Tables 3 and 4 to truly gauge their knowledge of
the AI concept. However, if we focus on children unfamiliar with
RL beforehand, there was a significant amount of them who gave
an accurate definition of RL post-application use. Although the
concept of RL and reward functions might still be abstract to these
individuals, we believe that this application provided them with an
intuition in this AI concept.

Conclusion

In this work, we introduced Reinforcement Learning to middle
school children by giving them the opportunity to train their own
AI model. Although we cannot conclude that there was any concrete
difference in understanding AI concepts, we can observe that 40
out of the 51 children not previously familiar with RL were able to
accurately describe it after using our software. Additionally, most
of the students indicated that they enjoyed training their own AI
using this TrainYourSnakeAI application. This work is part of an
effort to expose children to AI education and AI literacy.
Overall, while RL is a complex subject, efforts to introduce it at
various educational levels are increasing, supported by initiatives
that provide age-appropriate, engaging, and accessible learning
experiences. These initiatives highlight the importance of early
exposure to AI concepts to build a foundation for future learning
and innovation in the field.
