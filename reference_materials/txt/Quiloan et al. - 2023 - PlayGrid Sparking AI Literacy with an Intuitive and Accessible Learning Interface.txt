Abstract—Artiﬁcial Intelligence (AI) has become increasingly
commonplace for the average consumer, but AI is not commonly
understood. In a global survey of adults, 1/3 of users thought
they were using AI-powered services, but in reality, more than
3/4 of those consumers were using AI-powered technology. This
uninformed but unrealized use of AI hinders peoples’ capacity
to effectively utilize, cooperate with, and operate as critical
consumers of AI. There is a growing movement to develop AI
literacy in young children. This paper demonstrates a way to
introduce and familiarize young children with the Q-Learning
algorithm using the Adafruit NeoTrellis as an introductory step
in AI education. It is a fun, educational, and handheld gaming
device that has the potential for other AI-powered games to
support and increase AI literacy in young children.

I. I NTRODUCTION
Many people are unaware that they interact with artiﬁcial
intelligence (AI) daily. In 6000 adults surveyed globally,
only 33% of consumers thought they were using AI-powered
services, but in reality, 77% of those consumers were using
technology that featured AI [1]. The most common ways
consumers use AI in their day-to-day lives are through personalized marketing, voice assistants, navigation and traveling,
facial recognition technologies, and smart home devices [2].

II. R ELATED W ORK
A. AI Literacy
Literacy empowers individuals to express their selves and
communicate using written language [10]. It can enable an
individual from a marginalized or oppressed group to question
and challenge societal norms and transform their own lives
and the world around them [15]. There have been current,
growing efforts to teach literacy, primarily geared towards
non-technical individuals, in various computer science-related
skills known more broadly as computer science (CS) literacy
[10], [16]. [10] detailed more speciﬁc categories under CS
literacy, including digital literacy, which addresses proﬁciency
in using computational devices, computational literacy, the
ability to use code to articulate, investigate, and convey
concepts, and data literacy, the ability to interpret, manipulate,
examine, and debate using data within the broader context of
exploring the world. The authors deﬁned AI literacy as a “set
of competencies that enables individuals to critically evaluate
AI technologies; communicate and collaborate effectively with
AI; and use AI as a tool online, at home, and in the workplace”
[10]. The researchers argued that to have informed interactions
with AI, it is a critical ﬁrst step for people to recognize when
AI is in use. Furthermore, accurately understanding the current
capabilities, advantages, and limitations of AI enables people
to make better-informed decisions about how to properly
navigate an AI-entrenched environment [10].
In [16], researchers discovered that guiding 7 to 12-yearold children to develop custom machine learning models
and programs improved their understanding of smart device
limitations. The researchers found that the children “shifted
their attribution of agency from the devices to the people who
program them” [16]. Studies like [16] promote AI literacy in
a way that empowers individuals to fully comprehend that
they may exercise agency by making decisions and taking
actions. At the same time, AI still inﬂuences those individuals’
perceived autonomy over their decisions and actions [17]. [17]
argued that the amount of agency and autonomy in relation
to AI technology depends on factors like the transparency and
accessibility of the technology, as well as the individual’s own
AI literacy. AI literacy is an emerging topic in digital literacy
education research, as is in the following studies.
[12] identiﬁed key components of AI literacy and proposed
a framework to help individuals navigate the AI-driven world
effectively and responsibly. [11] described a Massachusetts
Institute of Technology (MIT) AI summer workshop designed
speciﬁcally for middle school students. [10] proposed a framework of AI literacy competencies and discussed design considerations for AI education. [17] identiﬁed AI literacy as one of
four new ethical principles employed that are unique to K-12
education. Finally, [18] presented an innovative pedagogical
model for AI literacy in early childhood education (ECE).

C. Reinforcement Learning
[22] described RL as a way of controlling agents through rewards and punishments without specifying how the task needs
to be achieved. [23] suggested that “states are the observations
that an agent or player sees and actions are the behaviors that
the agent takes in those states.” Essentially, states represent
observations, while the actions are the agent’s responses. RL
aims to learn how to achieve the highest possible reward in
the long term. Additionally, data is given in the form of stateaction pairs. Other ML models are typically trained on ﬁxed
datasets. The agent does not know or need to label the object
as an apple, for example. The agent simply learns that eating
the apple prolongs its life, so when encountering an apple, the
appropriate action is to eat it. The grid environment for the
PlayGrid described throughout this research is highly suitable
for the iterative learning process of RL, allowing agents to
effectively engage in trial and error to optimize their strategies.
[24] stated that in RL, an agent, which can be the AI, learns
from the environment by interacting with it through trial and
error and receiving positive or negative rewards as feedback
for performing actions. RL is “a computational approach of
learning from actions” [24]. One of the main beneﬁts of RL
is that, without human guidance, the algorithm interacts with
a dynamic environment where it performs different actions to
explore the best way to accomplish its task [23].
For example, in the video game Snake, the length of the
snake increases when it “eats” an apple. The “head” of the
snake continuously moves in a direction without stopping until
it is steered up, down, left, or right to avoid hitting the walls
or its own body. In this game, the apple is the reward. In any
given state, an agent sends outputs in the form of actions to
the environment, and the environment will respond with new
states and rewards if they exist. Furthermore, the reward may
be delayed until the future. More information on RL can be
found at [23] and [24].

1) Q-learning: [14] introduced Q-learning as a method for
iteratively estimating the optimal action-value function. [23]
stated that the Q-function takes two inputs – the agent’s current
state and the action executed while in that state. It returns the
expected total future sum of rewards that an agent can receive
after that point. If a really good action in the state is taken, the
Q-function returns a high expected total future reward, while
a bad action results in a low or penalized future reward [23].
Q-learning is a model-free reinforcement learning algorithm that can learn optimal actions in complex environments
without requiring a model of the environment. By iteratively
updating its action-value, or Q-value, estimates, the Q-learning
agent can eventually converge to an optimal policy, identifying
the best actions to take in each state to maximize cumulative
rewards [14].
[26] explained that in pathﬁnding tasks, the agent can use
the Q-learning algorithm to learn the shortest path or most
efﬁcient path between two points in an environment while
considering various factors such as obstacles. The researchers
argue that the algorithm’s ability to learn from trial-anderror experiences and adapt to changing environments makes
it suitable for pathﬁnding tasks in dynamic and unknown
environments. There have been many modiﬁcations to the Qlearning approach to improve performance [27]–[30]. The Qlearning algorithm is widely used in pathﬁnding mazes and
games [31], [26]-[28].

III. T HE P ROPOSED P LATFORMS
This paper started with an idea that evolved through three
different prototypes. Each prototype proposes the potential for
a system to introduce ML to young children.
The ﬁrst prototype, PlayGrid Mat, was initially envisioned
as a pallet on the ﬂoor with individually lit-up grids that
users could interact with by stepping, jumping, or dancing on.
Due to cost constraints, time-sensitive restrictions, and limited
programming skills, PlayGrid Sense was created. PlayGrid
Sense is more cost-effective and physically smaller, so a young
child can hold it. After demonstrating a proof-of-concept for
PlayGrid Sense, the lead author recognized that the interface
and user interaction were not satisfactory and the humancomputer interaction (HCI) component was weak. After more
research, the NeoTrellis by Adafruit was selected to improve
the system’s HCI, leading to the third prototype, PlayGrid Go.
It was more user-friendly and accessible to a young child.
Though each subsequent prototype was in response to either
the lead author’s or component’s limitation, each individual
prototype has the potential to be its own full-ﬂedged system.

1) PlayGrid Sense Design Goal: PlayGrid Sense was developed due to budget constraints, a growing development

Kinsley’s online tutorial [46] entailed creating a grid environment called Blobworld that utilized the Q-Learning algorithm. The environment had individually colored grids referred
to as “blobs.” The player blob is blue, the food blob is green,
and the enemy blob is red. The main goal of this program is for
the blue player blob to navigate its way as quickly as possible
to the green food blob while simultaneously avoiding the red
enemy blob. Kinsley recommends limiting the environment to
between 10x10 and 20x20. Environments larger than 20 x 20
result in a larger Q-Table that takes up more memory and
takes longer for the model to learn. As an example, a 10x10
Q -Table takes up about 15MB, and a 20x20 takes up about
195MB. In Kinsley’s program, the blobs move in four diagonal
directions – top-left, top-right, bottom-right, and bottom-left.
In this speciﬁc implementation, the agent is a ”blob” that
must navigate a 2D grid to ﬁnd food while avoiding enemies.
The Q-table is initialized with random values, and the agent
takes actions based on the highest Q-value for a given state.
The Q-values are updated based on the rewards received and
the expected future rewards.
