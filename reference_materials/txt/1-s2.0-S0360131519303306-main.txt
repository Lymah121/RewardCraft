This study examined the predictive impact of variables from self-regulated learning models on
transfer following multiple gameplay sessions in a classroom game-based learning environment
(GBLE). The game focused on science curriculum including map models, map navigation, and
landforms. Fifth-grade students (N ¼ 594) interacted with CRYSTAL ISLAND - UNCHARTED
DISCOVERY during six 50-min sessions over four weeks integrated with classroom instruction.
The transfer activity required students to create an island on grid paper that included at least
seven different landforms, a map navigation activity created for peers, and an attached map scale
model. Results of multiple regression analyses revealed that when other variables were accounted
for, none of the measured motivational variables including interest for the game, performanceapproach goal orientation, mastery-approach goal orientation, or self-efficacy for science were
significant positive predictors of transfer. Interestingly, video game self-efficacy was a significant
negative predictor of transfer performance. Prior knowledge and change in science knowledge
after gameplay were also significant predictors of transfer. Girls performed as well as boys on the
transfer task despite completing less game quests and had significantly lower posttest content
scores. Interest was a consistent predictor of performance for girls but not boys for in-game
performance, posttest content scores, and transfer scores. Regression models were systemati­
cally less able to predict performance moving from content knowledge, to in-game performance,
to transfer. Implications for the design of GBLEs to facilitate transfer are discussed.

1. Introduction
Recent evidence suggests that digital game-based learning environments (GBLEs) can have a range of positive cognitive, affective,
and motivational benefits (Clark, Tanner-Smith, & Killingsworth, 2016; Connolly, Boyle, MacArthur, Hainey, & Boyle, 2012; Tsai &
Tsai, 2018). GBLEs are touted because of the engaging nature by which they approach instruction (Prensky, 2001). Currently, how­
ever, there is a major challenge for researchers of GBLEs to demonstrate transfer of content and strategies outside of the game
environment (Hung & Van Eck, 2010; Mayer, 2011; Nietfeld, 2018). Yet, most studies of GBLEs are limited in their duration leaving
little potential to achieve transfer (Jackson & McNamara, 2013). Moreover, evidence for transfer has come mostly from near-transfer
test items or responses taken directly after gameplay with a high degree of alignment with the game itself (Pilegard & Mayer, 2016).
In order to demonstrate transfer outside of a game setting, students must effectively establish, monitor, and regulate academic goals
that are aligned with curricular objectives. This process requires effective self-regulated learning (SRL), or self-generated thoughts,
feelings, and actions for attaining one’s learning goals (Zimmerman and Moylan, 2009), and encompasses the coordination of
motivational, cognitive, and metacognitive processes. In traditional learning environments self-regulation has been linked with the

transfer of learning (Bannert & Reimann, 2012; Fuchs et al., 2003; Glaser & Brunstein, 2007; Zepeda, Richey, Ronevich, &
Nokes-Malach, 2015). However, in GBLEs the research in this regard is lacking. GBLEs have a high degree of potential to encourage
both SRL and transfer, yet they are particularly susceptible to extraneous processing that detracts from the desired learning experience
(Mayer, 2019). A pillar of SRL is the autonomy the learner is afforded and the opportunity to make critical choices in the learning
process (Pintrich, 2000). Digital GBLEs offer a high degree of autonomy as students are free to establish their own in-game goals, make
choices regarding how they proceed, and how they approach problems presented to them in the environment. To this end, learners can
be supported in GBLEs in ways not possible in traditional settings given the ability to utilize stealth assessment (Shute, 2011) that can
lead to customized, real-time scaffolds.
Thus, in order to advance the research momentum related to the academic benefits of GBLEs, it is important to begin to build sound
GBLEs that provide the potential for learning and subsequent transfer of that learning outside the game environment. In addition,
assessments that provide ecologically valid measures of transfer are also needed. These developments can then provide the context in
order to study game scaffolds and mechanics that promote transfer and also characteristics of the players themselves that promote
transfer. The current study is focused on the latter. The game employed in this study, Crystal Island – Uncharted Discovery, was
developed with mechanisms, further described below, that provided the potential for learners to both learn and subsequently transfer
their learning. The purpose of this study is to first describe a GBLE that was employed over four weeks in a fifth-grade classroom and
was equipped with opportunities, if utilized, that would encourage learning and the transfer of science content knowledge. Second, the
study reviews the process of integrating the GBLE into classrooms and then establishing an ecologically valid classroom measure of
transfer. The primary research questions then focused on the extent to which cognitive and motivational factors, typically found within
prevalent models of SRL, predicted successful transfer in a classroom activity external to the game environment. Given welldocumented gender differences related to digital games (Borgonovi, 2016; Hamlen, 2010; Homer, Hayward, Frye, & Plass, 2012;
Hsieh, Lin, & Hou, 2015), predictive models were also run separately by gender in order to develop an understanding of potential
differences that may impact modifications for future development of GBLEs.
1.1. Theoretical background
Transfer has been defined as the productive use of prior learning in a novel context (Pan & Rickard, 2018). Bransford, Brown, and
Cocking (2000) emphasize that transfer is an active rather than passive process and occurs more readily when knowledge is not overly
contextualized. Unfortunately, often times learners do not see the applicability of skills learned in academic contexts with new contexts
in which those skills might be applied. This problem likely arises from the fact that students perceive the skills they are learning as
being too domain-specific or irrelevant in other contexts. Achieving transfer has long been one of the grand challenges of traditional
education (Thorndike & Woodworth, 1901), but now it is at the forefront for digital learning environments as well. GBLEs, in
particular, face scrutiny for their ability to promote transfer given skepticism about their legitimacy as learning environments and
being perceived instead as motivational “fluff” (O’Neil, Wainess, & Baker, 2005). One recent review provided the answer of “maybe”
when referring to whether skills and knowledge from digital games transfer to educational settings (Hacker, 2017).
Adams, Mayer, MacNamara, Koenig, and Wainess (2012) questioned the efficacy of narrative GBLEs to promote transfer when
compared to more traditional approaches such as slideshow presentations. They suggested the need for extended gameplay for future
investigations. Much of Mayer and colleagues’ recent work has focused on how to encourage transfer using external aids outside of the
gaming platform. Fiorella and Mayer (2012) found advantages for paper-based aids that prompted focus on important game features
and underlying principles to promote transfer for college students on a basic circuit game. O’Neil, Chung, Kerr, Vendlinski, Buschang,
and Mayer (2014) found that self-explanation prompts could be beneficial for transfer with middle school students by connecting game
terminology with the curricular terminology (in this case mathematical concepts). Pilegard and Mayer (2016) utilized worksheets
external to the game of Cache-17, a game designed to teach concepts related to electric circuits, and found those used in the middle of
the game promoted transfer over a control condition and a pre-game worksheet condition. Moreno and Mayer (2005) found that
explanatory feedback but not reflection increased transfer for college students who played a game focused on plant survival under
different weather conditions. Similarly, Barzilai and Blau (2014) found advantages for an external scaffold with children aged 6–14 on
problem-solving measures outside of a business simulation game. These studies are encouraging but more research is needed to
examine transfer with self-contained games that do not require development of supporting materials or prompts.
There is limited evidence of transfer occurring with GBLEs in stand-alone contexts. Parong, Mayer, Fiorella, MacNamara, Homer, &
Plass (2017) were successful in finding transfer from a custom-designed video game, Alien Game, and the executive function skill of
shifting when players engaged in extended practice and practiced at a high level of challenge. Transfer was significantly correlated
with performance in the Crystal Island – Outbreak game and students who completed the game also scored significantly higher on
transfer measures (Nietfeld, Shores, & Hoffmann, 2014). Some GBLEs have the potential to promote transfer given that the envi­
ronment offers problem-solving through an applied and exploratory approach followed by expert feedback (Schwartz, Chase, Oppezzo,
& Chin, 2011). However, the challenge remains for students to gain a full understanding of the underlying structure of the targeted
problems and content in order to be able to apply their newfound knowledge in a context outside of the game (Gick & Holyoak, 1983).
The extent to which learners learn academic content from a GBLE or make attempts to transfer knowledge may largely be
dependent on exhibiting effective SRL. Prevalent cognitive and social-cognitive models of SRL (Pintrich, 2000; Winne & Hadwin, 1998;
Zimmerman, 2000) emphasize cognitive, metacognitive, and motivational processes that impact learning within “episodes” experi­
enced by the learner (Winne, 2010). Common across theories is the understanding that within these episodes learners engage in initial
planning or forethought processes, then monitoring and regulation during task involvement, and finally some form of evaluation. SRL
is malleable and should be conceptualized as a matter of degree where all learners exhibit some degree of self-regulation, however

more effective students have much greater skills. Moreover, SRL has been shown to be related to effective learning and achievement
outcomes (Cleary & Kitsantas, 2017; Pintrich & De Groot, 1990; Yang, Chen, & Chen, 2018). From a SRL perspective, Hacker (2017)
has argued the need for the games to assist the player in developing agency and to experience interactivity, choice, and control while at
the same time providing guidance for the development of metacognitive skills that will facilitate transfer.
The emergence of GBLEs came with parallel assumptions that GBLEs’ primary advantage over more traditional forms of instruction
was their motivational appeal. However, the extent to which this is a valid empirical claim is yet to be fully determined as serious
games have been shown to have stronger benefits for learning than motivation (Wouters, van Nimwegen, Oostendorp, & van der Spek,
2013). A number of motivation variables included within models of SRL have been increasingly introduced in the study of serious
games. For instance, Mayer (2019) suggests that five classes of motivational theories that contribute to the motivational power of
games: interest and value theories, self-efficacy and attribution theories, goal orientation theory, self-determination and intrinsic
motivation theories, and social cue and embodiment theories. Variables from the first three of these theories were included in the
current study. The first, situational interest, is considered to be a gateway to more long-lasting personal interest in that it can be the
initiating effect that leads to continued participation and increased levels of interest over time (Schraw & Lehman, 2001). In GBLEs,
situational interest has been shown to be higher for students who play video games versus engage in traditional instruction (Hwang,
Wu, & Chen, 2012) and also higher within competitive and collaborative as opposed to individual conditions (Plass et al., 2013).
The second, self-efficacy, can be defined as one’s confidence in their ability to plan and implement a course of action to solve a
problem or accomplish a task (Bandura, 1997). Self-efficacy has been shown to be predictive of performance in GBLEs (Nietfeld et al.,
2014) and improve as the result of playing GBLEs (Meluso, Zheng, Spires, & Lester, 2012; Sung & Hwang, 2013). The current study
examined both a measure of self-efficacy for playing video games and also for learning science.
The third motivational variable is goal orientation that focuses on two primary orientations corresponding to mastery and per­
formance (Ames & Archer, 1988; Dweck, 1986; Elliot & McGregor, 2001). Mastery-approach individuals are focused on gaining
knowledge, increasing ability, and incrementally gaining skill. Performance-approach individuals are interested in performing well
relative to their peers. In traditional learning environments unique benefits have been show by both orientations (Harackiewicz,
Barron, & Elliot, 1998) as mastery goals have been associated with higher levels of cognitive strategy use (Wolters, Yu, & Pintrich,
1996), metacognitive strategy use (Wolters, 2004), interest (Wolters et al., 1996), and cognitive engagement and effort (Meece,
Blumenfeld, & Hoyle, 1988). Performance goals, on the other hand, have been linked to various outcome measures of achievement
across studies (Senko, Hulleman, & Harackiewicz, 2011). The role of goal orientation in GBLEs is not yet clearly understood, however
one might posit that it could determine the emphasis one places on achieving points and in-game rewards versus gaining content
mastery while also influencing the extent to which one persists in attempting to solve a complex problem.
In the current study, measures of prior content knowledge and problem-solving processes were also included to represent cognition.
The activation of prior knowledge is critical to SRL (Alexander, Dinsmore, Parkinson, & Winters, 2011; Alexander, Schallert, & Hare,
1991) and the framing of that knowledge in the learning process itself (Engle, Lam, Meyer, & Nix, 2012) and the ability of the learner to
abstract general principals and apply them to new settings (Gick & Holyoak, 1983) is critical for transfer of learning. Prior knowledge is
critical to assess when examining SRL to account for learner differences in knowledge and also because prior knowledge may influence
SRL and performance in unique ways across domains (Alexander et al., 2011). SRL and prior knowledge are intertwined in that the
development of new knowledge is dependent upon effective self-regulation and the quality of self-regulation and performance is highly
dependent upon prior knowledge. Problem-solving scenarios are central to GBLEs and require the learner to engage in metacognitive
monitoring and regulation activities (Kim, Park, & Baek, 2009). Presentation of a general problem-solving model to work from has
been shown to increase academic problem-solving performance for novices (Youssef-Shalala, Ayres, Schubert, & Sweller, 2014).
One area of investigation related to transfer in GBLEs with very little research up to this point is the impact of gender on the transfer
of learning. What is known is that boys exhibit higher self-efficacy for games (Hartmann & Klimmt, 2011) and spend more time playing
games than girls (Hamlen, 2010). Moreover, both boys and girls appear to benefit from GBLEs on content knowledge (Nietfeld et al.,
2014). Yet, an open question for the field is how gender impacts the process of attempting to transfer learning outside of GBLEs. Do
varied gender-related experiences with GBLEs impact the process of achieving transfer outside GBLEs?

1.2. Current study
The current study describes efforts to measure and predict transfer from one GBLE that was integrated into classroom instruction.
Crystal Island – Uncharted Discovery (see Fig. 1) is an immersive GBLE for 5th graders described more fully in prior work (Lester et al.,
2014). In the game, students play the role of a shipwrecked passenger who is stranded on a volcanic island. The overall goal is to solve a
series of quests (7 total) in order to find a means to get off of the island. The quests are self-contained game-like activities focusing on
landform identification, map navigation, and modeling (topics aligned with state science curriculum), respectively, and are leveled
based on difficulty. The students are free to complete the quests in any order they please; however, students must successfully complete
the first level of all quests before engaging in any of the second level quests. A number of scaffolds are available to assist in problem
solving including maps, landform experts who happened to be among the ship-wrecked crew, a tablet equipped with note-taking tools,
a camera, a log to monitor quest completion and progress, a glossary of key landform and map skill terminology, and a problem-solving
application.
Crystal Island – Uncharted Discovery is fully self-contained therefore external materials or cues to encourage transfer were not
included in the study. The interface of the game encouraged the integration and application of conceptual knowledge within the quests
and provided tools. For example, metacognitive prompts were provided using a simulated tablet to cue the students to check their
understanding, keep notes, access additional information, communicate with scientists in the game, and keep track of tasks. The game
employed in the current study was designed and tested by an inter-disciplinary team over the course of four years. The current study
utilized an extended gameplay approach to report on the culminating integration of the game over a four-week time period.
Two primary research questions were explored to examine learner variables that predicted success on the transfer activity. What
motivational and cognitive variables predict transfer in a science GBLE? Given the Wouters et al. (2013) meta-analytic finding that GBLEs
have thus far failed to support motivation more than conventional instructional condition it is important to turn the directionality of
influence around and examine how motivation impacts learning in GBLEs. Specifically, this study examined the unique contributions
of motivational and cognitive variables, prevalent in models of SRL, in predicting transfer of learning outside the GBLE. It was not
possible to measure all motivational variables important for SRL in the current study, however those chosen are generally considered
to be fundamental within current models of SRL and critical when assessing motivation in GBLEs (Mayer, 2019; Pintrich, 2000; Winne
& Hadwin, 1998; Zimmerman, 2000). In order to test this question a multiple regression approach was undertaken in order to examine
the unique effects of prior knowledge, changes in knowledge that took place during the duration of gameplay, pretest knowledge of
problem solving, and motivational variables including self-efficacy, goal orientation, and interest with a large sample of students. In

2. Method
2.1. Participants
A total of 594 students (97% were between 10 and 11 years old; 52% female) across eight schools participated. Intact classrooms
agreed to participate and include Crystal Island – Uncharted Discovery as part of their regular classroom instruction. The schools,
teachers, and students chosen for the study had agreed to participate more broadly in a multi-year project aimed at developing Crystal
Island – Uncharted Discovery. However, the specific students in the current study had not participated in the game prior to this study.
The students were represented by the following ethnic breakdown: American Indian/Alaskan native 1.3%; Asian 7.7%; Black/African
American 12.8%; Hawaiian/Pacific Islander .3%; Hispanic/Latino 5.2%; White 65.5%; Multiracial 7.1%.
2.2. Materials
Content Knowledge Test. This was measured with a research team-constructed, 19-item multiple-choice test that was based on state
curriculum and designed to measure domain-related material integrated within Crystal Island – Uncharted Discovery. The same items
were used as a test of prior knowledge (α ¼ 0.80) and as a posttest measure (α ¼ 0.78). To avoid test/retest learning no feedback was
provided on item correctness on the pretest. The items ranged in requirement from knowledge/definitional level to application-level
items (see Fig. 2 below). An additional variable was constructed to measure change from the pretest to the posttest as an indication of
in-game learning. Additional examples of both knowledge/definitional items and an application item are provided in Appendix A.
Gaming Self-Efficacy. Video game self-efficacy was measured using a four-item scale (α ¼ 0.79). For example, one item asked, “I am
certain that I can master any video game I play.” The Gaming Survey was given as a pretest.
AGQ (Achievement Goals Questionnaire): This is a 12-item scale that measures achievement goal orientation in the form of four
factors: mastery approach, mastery avoidance, performance approach, and performance avoidance (Elliot & McGregor, 2001). For this
study the mastery approach (α ¼ 0.86) and performance approach (α ¼ 0.83) facets were included in the analysis.
Situational Interest. At the end of Crystal Island – Uncharted Discovery participants were asked to rate their interest in the game with
10 items adapted from the Perceived Interest Questionnaire (Schraw, 1997). For example, one item asks “Did you enjoy this game?”

Science Self-Efficacy. A science learning self-efficacy inventory was given as a pretest that included three items each for landforms

first four items asked students to correctly order steps of a general problem solving approach (Polya, 1957) and for the last four items
students were provided with a fictitious problem (create a school map for a new student at your school) and asked to provide the
correct order of approaches for solving the problem that represented steps of the general problem-solving model.
2.3. Procedures
The study took place over four weeks (see Table 1). Participating classroom teachers first attended a one-day professional
development workshop to discuss plans for the integration of Crystal Island – Uncharted Discovery into their curriculum. The schedule
included six 50-min classroom sessions playing the game that took place in parallel with six traditional classroom lessons that aligned
with the curriculum in the game. While classroom effects could not be eliminated in the current study, steps were taken to minimize
those effects. For example, gameplay within Crystal Island – Uncharted Discovery was self-contained and required no interaction with
teachers. Moreover, for the supplemental in-class lessons, lesson plans and scripts were created and agreed upon by the participating
teachers and project research team and made accessible to all teachers on a project website.
Creation of the transfer measure was undertaken by carefully matching the Crystal Island – Uncharted Discovery curriculum with
the requirements of the task and presented as a Final Inquiry project completed during the last week of the study. The task required
students to display mastery of landforms, map navigation, and map models. To ensure content validity participating classroom
teachers were provided with the task beforehand and asked to provide feedback. The task directions asked students to draw an outline
of an island on a large sheet of grid paper. They were provided with colored pencils and asked to include seven landforms on their
island that included a mountain, river, waterfall, plateau, delta, lake, and tributary. Students were instructed that they could also
include other landforms and landmarks as long as they included the seven required landforms. They were asked to consider the
location of their landforms and also the accuracy of their drawings as they drew. For map navigation, students were asked to select
three locations where they could hide secret treasures. However, they were instructed not to draw the treasures. Instead, they were
asked to create three clues of increasing difficulty that they could give to their peers as a game. The instructions were as follows:
Clue #1: On the blank sheet of paper provided, write a clue or set of clues using at least one grid coordinate that could help a
classmate find your first hidden treasure.
Clue #2: Using a landmark or landform as a starting point, write a clue or set of clues using compass directions and the map scale to
help a classmate locate your second hidden treasure.
Clue #3: Using a landmark or landform as a starting point, write a clue or set of clues using compass directions, the map scale, and
the map grid to help a classmate locate your third hidden treasure.
For map models, students were provided with a smaller grid and asked to draw a small model of their island. They were instructed

After the maps were created students exchanged their large maps and clue sheets with a classmate while keeping their small model

A graduate student oversaw a team of four teachers hired to score the maps. The group used a rigorous scoring and reliability
process that included the creation of a scoring rubric. Each project was scored for the inclusion of landforms on the map and key,
accurate drawing and location of landforms, and on the construction of accurate problems and answers for a total of 28 possible points
(M ¼ 20.60; SD ¼ 4.35; range ¼ 4–28). Two raters scored each map and key. Discrepancies between raters were discussed until arriving
at an agreed upon score. These results suggest that overall students were able to transfer a substantial amount of the content from the
game to an external task. Students were most successful in constructing components of their map (e.g., including accurate landforms
and accurate key) as they scored 81% of the total possible points. Their greatest challenge came in creating accurate map navigation
problems and clues for their peers to complete as they scored only 45% of the total possible points. A sample student island (Fig. 3) and

2.4. Data analysis
The primary data in the current study consisted of responses from self-report inventories, in-game trace measures of quests
completed, performance scores on a science content test, and scores on a performance-based measure of transfer completed in a
classroom context. For the measure of transfer, rubrics were created and subsequently applied using multiple raters to determine
scores. Primary analysis was conducted using multiple regression in order to examine the unique effects of prior knowledge, changes in
knowledge that took place during the duration of gameplay, pretest knowledge of problem solving, and motivational variables
including self-efficacy, goal orientation, and interest with a large sample of students. The large sample and multiple regression
approach allowed for estimates of the amount of variation accounted for in the predictor variables as a whole to indicate how well
transfer can be explained from multiple sources. Multiple models were run to predict science content knowledge, in-game perfor­
mance, and transfer to provide comparisons with regard to the relative ability overall to predict each outcome measure.
3. Results

An examination of the impact of gameplay on science content knowledge was first accomplished using pre and post science tests.
Results revealed significant gains (Cohen’s d ¼ 0.71) in science content knowledge from pre (M ¼ 11.93, SD ¼ 3.97) to posttest (M ¼
13.81, SD ¼ 3.52).
Both boys (Mpretest ¼ 12.32; SD ¼ 3.86; Mposttest ¼ 14.21; SD ¼ 3.21; t ¼ 12.49; p < .001, Cohen’s d ¼ 0.75) and girls (Mpretest ¼
11.57; SD ¼ 4.04; Mposttest ¼ 13.45; SD ¼ 3.74; t ¼ 11.74; p < .001, Cohen’s d ¼ 0.67) showed significant increases in science content
knowledge after gameplay. Boys scored significantly higher on the content posttest (t ¼ 2.65; p ¼ .008, Cohen’s d ¼ 0.21), however, no
significant differences remained between genders on posttest knowledge when controlling for pretest scores. Yet, boys completed more
game quests even after controlling for game time (p < .001; η2p ¼ 0.03). No significant differences were found between boys (M ¼ 20.10;
SD ¼ 4.25) and girls (M ¼ 21.05; SD ¼ 4.40) on the transfer task.
3.1. What cognitive and motivational variables predict transfer in a science GBLE?
A multiple regression analysis was conducted to predict scores on the transfer task. The model included the following predictor
variables: science prior knowledge, change in knowledge from the pre to posttest, problem solving, video game self-efficacy, selfefficacy for science content, interest for Crystal Island – Uncharted Discovery, mastery approach goal orientation, and performance
approach goal orientation. A number of assumptions regarding the analysis and data were tested. First, data was examined for outliers
using standardized residuals and no cases were found (Std. Residual Min ¼ 3.17, Std. Residual Max ¼ 1.87) using 3.29 and 3.29 as
thresholds (Field, 2013). Next, tests for the assumption of collinearity indicated no concerns (Tolerance range from 0.46 to 0.97; VIF
range from 1.05 to 2.20). Furthermore, the data met the assumption of non-zero variances (Video Game Self-Efficacy variance ¼ 13.84,
Problem-Solving variance ¼ 1.40, Mastery Approach variance ¼ 20.65, Performance Approach variance ¼ 38.73, Science Prior
Knowledge variance ¼ 15.75, Knowledge Change variance ¼ 7.23, Science Self-Efficacy variance ¼ 15.62, Crystal Island interest
variance ¼ 63.33; Map Transfer task variance ¼ 18.94).
When predicting transfer the model was significant F(8,559) ¼ 11.13, R2 ¼ 0.137, p < .001 (see Table 3). Within the model, science
prior knowledge (β ¼ 0.363, p < .001), change in knowledge (β ¼ 0.170, p < .001), and pre-study video game self-efficacy (β ¼ 0.101,
p ¼ .011) were all significant predictors. Both prior knowledge and change in knowledge were positive predictors of transfer whereas
video game self-efficacy was a negative predictor. None of the motivational variables were significant predictors of transfer.

4. Discussion
This study focused on player characteristics within a SRL framework that predicted transfer of learning from a GBLE focused on
science in 5th grade classrooms. Students who played Crystal Island – Uncharted Discovery over four weeks demonstrated significant
learning gains. These gains coupled with the creation and implementation of a transfer measure that garnered support from the
participating teachers was encouraging. The study contributes to the existing GBLE literature in that it is one of the first to examine
how key motivational and cognitive SRL variables predict transfer with a large sample of students (N ¼ 594) after an extended period
of classroom integration. It is also one of the first studies to examine these predictors of transfer in GBLEs with a particular focus on
gender. Major findings and implications for the major study questions related to predicting transfer follow below.
From a criterion-based perspective, students appeared to be successful in transferring their knowledge of landforms and application
skills in creating a map and corresponding scale. Understandably, however, students had more difficulty creating the map navigation
game for their peers. While not measured in the current study, one might posit that students who were “surface focusers” may have
needed additional support either within or outside of the game or both in order to be successful in applying their knowledge in the

current study is likely due to the fact that Crystal Island – Uncharted Discovery provided simulated performance-based quests at two
levels for each content area. The quests functioned to break the larger problem down into manageable units and provide frequent
feedback (Shores, Hoffmann, Nietfeld, & Lester, 2012).
The first research question examined the ability of motivational and cognitive variables, prevalent in models of SRL, in their ability
to predict transfer. Surprisingly, across the sample as a whole, no motivation variables were significant positive predictors of transfer.
Relatedly, meta-analysis by Wouters et al. (2013) found that serious games were not more motivating than conventional instructional
methods. However, the current study approached motivation from a different perspective: the impact of players’ motivation on the
transfer of learning – but with the same general finding that motivation variables, at least those included in this study, appeared to have
little direct impact on the transfer of learning. The motivational variables employed in this study were certainly not exhaustive but
represented three of the five dimensions of motivation that contribute toward the motivational power of games according to Mayer
(2019). Following prevalent approaches by prior studies, all of the motivational measures were self-report. Given the known limi­
tations of such measures (Winne, 2010) trace-based measures and physiological indicators of motivation may have yielded different
results and should be considered in future studies. It is also possible that motivational variables such as self-efficacy, goal-orientation,
and interest are critical to engage learners in GBLE activities and may influence transfer in more indirect pathways, but that the
development of knowledge structures allowing for cognitive flexibility is primary in the direct relationship of knowledge transfer
(Spiro, Vispoel, Schmitz, Samarapungavan, & Boerger, 1987).
The inverse relationship between video game self-efficacy and transfer could be interpreted in multiple ways. One explanation
could be a general level of overconfidence by those students who have had more experience playing video games. Another potential
explanation could be a disconnect in expectations for commercial versus serious games. Performance in Crystal Island – Uncharted
Discovery emphasized the learning, understanding, and application of content knowledge over and above action-based motor skills
developed by the players. Even though the game was being presented in a school setting, this was likely not well understood by
students when they considered their reference points when making self-efficacy judgments. The finding of change in knowledge as a
significant predictor suggests that students who made the most learning gains within the game were also those most likely to transfer
their knowledge outside of the game. This finding is promising for the potential of games such as Crystal Island – Uncharted Discovery to
facilitate transfer. Numerous other unaccounted for effects, namely the parallel classroom instruction, could have impacted this effect,
yet this study presents initial findings that should lead to more controlled follow up studies. Indeed, however, only 14% of the total
variance for predicting transfer performance was accounted for in the model.
The second research question in the current study examined the separate profiles of transfer-related performance by gender. Girls
performed as well as boys on the transfer task even though they had completed significantly less game quests and had significantly
lower posttest content scores. Regression models for both genders were systematically less able to predict performance moving from
content knowledge, to game performance, to transfer indicating the challenge facing researchers in identifying successful pathways to
transfer. Not surprisingly, prior knowledge was a consistently strong predictor of content knowledge, in-game performance, and
transfer across genders. The finding that stands out the most is the fact that even through there were no differences between genders for
interest (t ¼ 0.32, p ¼ .75), interest was a consistent predictor of performance for girls across all three regression models but not for
boys. In prior studies, positive relationships between science interest and performance have been found for both genders and cultures,
although smaller in magnitude than mathematics (Evans, Schweingruber, & Stevenson, 2002). Separate analyses by gender in the
Author (20xxb) study failed to show situational interest as a significant predictor of in-game performance in either model. Given the
limited prior work in this area, it appears that direct comparisons to other studies predicting transfer by gender, particularly in GBLEs,
is unavailable to date. If findings from the current study are replicated and generalize across contexts the implications imply a need to
assess interest in the GBLE early on in the user experience, particularly for girls. This also includes the need to carefully consider
features of the GBLE itself during the process of development that would likely promote interest for girls.
Results of the current study reveal a challenge for predicting transfer for boys as only prior knowledge was a significant in the
model. Thus, researchers must delve deeper and consider more trace-based measures or other real-time data-tracking methods that are
more sensitive to the process of transfer during the duration of time students are playing the game.
Interestingly, problem-solving was a significant predictor of content knowledge and quests completed for both genders yet was not

significant for either gender in predicting transfer. The ‘Create an Island’ transfer task was essentially a problem-based learning activity
that required effective implementation of steps from the general problem-solving model assessed by the problem-solving pretest.
Nevertheless, the problem-solving measure appeared to have a greater relative impact on content knowledge gains and in-game
performance. The negative relationship between girls’ science self-efficacy and their content knowledge gain is counter to limited
prior research from similar GBLE contexts (Bergey, Ketelhut, Liang, Natarajan, & Karakus, 2015). Girls’ judgments may have been in
reference to more typical learning environments rather than from a GBLE in which they have less prior experience than their male
peers. Similarly, performance approach was not a significantly positive predictor of performance in any of the six models but was a
significantly negative predictor of quests completed for boys. One possibility for this finding is the lack of norm-based performance
feedback. Crystal Island – Uncharted Discovery did include mastery-based recognition in the form of levels of trophies, however these did
not provide performance-approach feedback allowing for comparison of performance relative to others. An interesting potential follow
up might consider adding real-time measures that provide comparative indices between players that promote competition (Plass et al.,
2013). Mastery approach, on the other hand, was a significant predictor of content knowledge for both boys and girls.
4.1. Implications for educators and designers of educational games
In addition to those discussed above, there are a number of implications from this study for educators and designers of educational
games to consider. With regard to the negative relationship between video-game self-efficacy and transfer, educators may consider
measures that ask students to consider their efficacy to learn and apply academic content outside of the game environment. If the
negative relationship persists with these, presumably, more sensitive measures, one approach would be to develop mechanisms in the
game environment to periodically check for calibration, or the ability to match one’s confidence with performance, and to subse­
quently deliver appropriate scaffolds to increase calibration. Another important implication relates to the strong influence revealed by
both prior knowledge and the change in knowledge during gameplay to predict transfer. Given this finding, developers of education
games would be encouraged to work closely with classroom teachers to consider means to develop knowledge that can be ‘flexibly
packaged’ within the game narrative with the intent of increasing the transfer of that knowledge outside the game. Relatedly, findings
in the current study would support game designers including elements to encourage mastery approaches to learning in the game.
Finally, given findings such as interest predicting girls’ performance across the board, developers should be encouraged to work closely
with teachers to ensure intake of diagnostic information early on in game interaction in order to make needed adaptations. This
approach would likely also get at meaningful patterns that emerge beyond that of gender that were not accounted for in the current
study.
4.2. Limitations
While the current study makes a unique contribution to the literature with a rigorously designed transfer measure integrated in an
actual classroom, the major limitation was the lack of a comparison condition that did not employ a GBLE. Use of a comparison
condition would have allowed for a more direct measure of the impact of a GBLE on transfer relative to ‘business as usual’ science
instruction. Follow on studies should consider experimental designs following best practices for assessing GBLEs (All, Castellar, & Van
Looy, 2016) by creating and describing comparable experimental and control conditions that vary only in the use of the GBLE itself.
4.3. Future studies
Future studies should examine more closely the variables that directly impact transfer in GBLEs. This will require a greater degree
of control across conditions to isolate targeted components that may influence transfer of learning. Given the critical importance for
students to be able to transfer their learning widely across contexts, future studies should also test the generalization of the current
findings across game platforms and content domains and also to examine the mechanisms for transfer more deeply. The value and
sustainability of serious game depends largely on the efficacy of such games to facilitate the display of student learning outside of the
gaming context.
